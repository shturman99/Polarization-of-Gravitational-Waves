\section{Introduction}
Gravitational waves provide a unique probe of high-energy physics and physical processes in the early universe \cite{Dent:2025}: they test highest energy scales inaccessible by any laboratory experiments and far beyond the epoch probed on cosmic microwave background (CMB), see Ref.~\cite{Roshan:2025} for a recent review and references therein. 
%
Several different sources of gravitational radiation are potentially present during the early stages of the universe expansion: these sources include (but are not limited by) quantum-mechanical perturbations present during and short after the inflationary epoch, 
bubble collisions and nucleation during first order cosmological phase transitions 
and the follow up sound waves and/or turbulent motions, see Ref.~\cite{Caprini:2018mtu} and references therein. Turbulence of primordial plasma can be induced without first order transitions, for example, through the presence of the magnetic seeds (and vise versa) \cite{Brandenburg:1996fc,Enqvist:1998fw,Son:1998my},  and/or through 
small inhomogeneities leading to the pressure gradient \cite{Miniati:2017kah},  
and/or presence of any kind of anisotropic stress \cite{Deryagin:19886}.  
%
An exceptionally interesting case is related to sources that violate parity (mirror) symmetry: the standard model does not explain the predominance of matter over antimatter,  and explanations, such as leptogenesis and baryogenesis, hypothesize that there could be parity violation occurring at high temperatures and leading to matter-antimatter asymmetry \cite{Fukugita:1986hr}. A key feature of the induced gravitational wave signal is its polarization, which carries information about parity-violating processes in the early universe \cite{Riotto:1999yt}.
%
Actually, the CMB anisotropies observations can also be used to probe parity violation from the early universe,  through birefrigence and parity-odd cross correlations of temperature and polarization anisotropies \cite{Lue:1998mq}. 
However, other processes (for example, Lorentz symmetry violation) can mimic parity violation \cite{Kostelecky:2001mb} and additionally there are observational challenges associated with CMB foregrounds, such as dust masking the signal \cite{Diego-Palazuelos:2022dsq}, 
gravitational lensing that is not intrinsically present \cite{Zaldarriaga:1998ar}. 
Contrary to these difficulties of probing parity symmetry violation in the early universe through its traces on the CMB, the stochastic gravitational wave background polarization provides (almost) undamaged  patterns of intrinsic parity breaking. 


In this paper, we consider analytic and simulation-based phenomenological approaches to studying the polarization of gravitational waves from helical (parity violating) primordial hydro- and magnetohydrodynamical (MHD) turbulence possibly present after inflationary stage but not later than the recombination epoch. Our models assume that turbulence has been generated, but are agnostic to the source of turbulence. To keep our description as general as possible, we will consider different models of turbulence (such as with and without helicity, stationary and decaying, etc).

\subsection{Polarization}
For the purposes of this work, we have the following metric.
\begin{equation}
    ds^{2}=-dt^{2}+a(t)(\delta_{ij}+h_{ij}(t,\vec{x}))dx^{i}dx^{j}
\end{equation}

The metric perturbations can be expressed as plane waves:

\begin{equation} h_{ij}(t, \vec{x}) = \sum_{\lambda} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} df \int d^2 \n
e^{2i\pi f(t - \n \cdot \vec{x})} e^{\lambda}_{ij}(\n) h_{\lambda}(f, \n) \end{equation}

where $\lambda = {+,\times}$ represents the two polarization states of the gravitational wave. These modes clearly depend only on the direction $\n$ of the gravitational wave, but there is also a dependence on the parameters frequency of the modes. This frequency dependence, as assumed in parameters\cite{2023AnilKumar}, applies to an isotropic stochastic gravitational wave background. 

\begin{equation}
    \langle h_{\lambda}(f,\hat{n})h_{\lambda'}^{*}(f',\hat{n}) \rangle =\delta (f-f')\delta(\hat{n}-\hat{n}')\mathcal{P}_{\lambda\lambda'}(f)
    % maek sure that normalization is correct $4\pi$
\end{equation}
\begin{equation*}
\left\langle\tilde{h}_\lambda(f, \hat{n}) \tilde{h}_{\lambda^{\prime}}^*(f^{\prime}, \hat{n}^{\prime})\right\rangle=\frac{1}{4} \mathcal{P}(f, \hat{n}) \delta\left(f-f^{\prime}\right) \delta_{\lambda \lambda^{\prime}} \delta^2(\hat{n}, \hat{n}^{\prime}),    
\end{equation*}

The $\mathcal{P}_{\lambda\lambda'}$ is a spectra density and its dependence on the polarization states of the gravitational wave can be expressed using Stokes's parameters:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{P}_{p, p^{\prime}}(f, \hat{\Omega})=\left(
\begin{array}{cc}
I(f, \hat{\Omega})+Q(f, \hat{\Omega}) & U(f, \hat{\Omega})-i V(f, \hat{\Omega}) \\
U(f, \hat{\Omega})+i V(f, \hat{\Omega}) & I(f, \hat{\Omega})-Q(f, \hat{\Omega})
\end{array}\right)
\end{equation}
with the following definitions of each parameter:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
& I(f, \hat{n})=\frac{1}{2}\left\langle | \tilde{h}_{+}|^2+|\tilde{h}_{\times}|^2\right\rangle \\
& Q(f, \hat{n})=\frac{1}{2}\left\langle | \tilde{h}_{+}|^2-|\tilde{h}_{\times}|^2\right\rangle \\
& U(f, \hat{n})=\operatorname{Re}\left\langle\tilde{h}_{+}^* \tilde{h}_{\times}\right\rangle=\frac{1}{2}\left\langle\tilde{h}_{+}^* \tilde{h}_{\times}+\tilde{h}_{\times}^* \tilde{h}_{+}\right\rangle \\
& V(f, \hat{n})=\operatorname{Im}\left\langle\tilde{h}_{+}^* \tilde{h}_{\times}\right\rangle=\frac{1}{2 i}\left\langle\tilde{h}_{+}^* \tilde{h}_{\times}-\tilde{h}_{\times}^* \tilde{h}_{+}\right\rangle
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The usefulness of this decomposition is the explicit transformations of this quantities under rotations:
\begin{equation}
\begin{gathered}
I(\hat{n})=I_0 \sum_{L=0}^{\infty} \sum_{M=-L}^L c_{L M}^I Y_{L M}(\hat{n}), \\
V(\hat{n})=I_0 \sum_{L=0}^{\infty} \sum_{M=-L}^L c_{L M}^V Y_{L M}(\hat{n}), \\
P_{ \pm}(\hat{n})=I_0 \sum_{L=4}^{\infty} \sum_{M=-L}^L c_{L M \pm 4}^{ \pm} Y_{L M}(\hat{n}),
\end{gathered}
\end{equation}
where $P_{\pm}=Q\pm iU$. Here we see that $I$ and $V$ transform like scalars while $P_{\pm}$ transforms like spin-4 file.\\
Conversion from the linear polarization basis ($+$, $\times$) typically used in gravitational wave (gravitational wave) analyses, to the helical polarization basis, which consists of left-handed (L) and right-handed (R) circular polarization states. This helical basis is particularly useful when analyzing parity-violating phenomena or when clearly isolating circular polarization components.

We start by defining the relationship between the linear polarization tensors and the helical polarization tensors as done in the appendix B of \cite{2019Domcke}. For a gravitational wave propagating in the direction $\hat{n}$, the polarization tensors can be transformed as follows:

\begin{align}
&e^{R}_{ij}(\hat{n}) = \frac{e^{+}_{ij}(\hat{n}) + i e^{\times}_{ij}(\hat{n})}{\sqrt{2}}, \\
&e^{L}_{ij}(\hat{n}) = \frac{e^{+}_{ij}(\hat{n}) - i e^{\times}_{ij}(\hat{n})}{\sqrt{2}}
\end{align}

Conversely, we express the linear polarization tensors in terms of the helical polarization tensors:

\begin{equation}
e^{+}_{ij}(\hat{n}) = \frac{e^{R}_{ij}(\hat{n}) + e^{L}_{ij}(\hat{n})}{\sqrt{2}}, \quad
e^{\times}_{ij}(\hat{n}) = \frac{e^{R}_{ij}(\hat{n}) - e^{L}_{ij}(\hat{n})}{\sqrt{2}i}
\end{equation}

The gravitational wave mode expansions in the helical basis become:

\begin{equation}
h_{ij}(t, \vec{x}) = \sum_{\lambda=R,L} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} df \int d^2 \hat{n}
e^{2\pi i f(t - \hat{n} \cdot \vec{x})} e^{\lambda}{ij}(\hat{n}) h_{\lambda}(f, \hat{n})
\end{equation}

The power spectral densities in the helical basis can be expressed analogously to the linear case, with the Stokes parameters now relating directly to the helical amplitudes.

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
&I(f, \hat{n}) = \frac{1}{2}\left\langle |\tilde{h}_{R}|^2 + |\tilde{h}_{L}|^2 \right\rangle, \\
&V(f, \hat{n}) = \frac{1}{2}\left\langle |\tilde{h}_{R}|^2 - |\tilde{h}_{L}|^2 \right\rangle, \\
&Q(f, \hat{n}) + iU(f, \hat{n}) = \left\langle \tilde{h}_{R}\tilde{h}_{L}^* \right\rangle.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Here, $I$ and $V$ are directly interpreted as the total intensity and the circular polarization, respectively, while $Q$ and $U$ measure linear polarization correlations within the helical framework. This decomposition facilitates explicit studies of gravitational wave polarization properties, particularly under rotations and boosts, relevant for detecting and characterizing anisotropic or polarized stochastic gravitational wave backgrounds.
